<TldrCallout>
Bai, Philippon & Savov (2016) ask whether equity prices have become better predictors of future firm performance. Their answer is yes: the informativeness of the S&P 500 cross-section has trended upward since the 1960s. This replication recovers 29 of 44 published metrics (66%); all 15 failures trace to a single irrecoverable data-vintage issue — `comp.idxcst_his` was stripped of pre-1964 historical S&P 500 membership data by S&P Global after publication, leaving 87 early permnos with no Compustat link. Cross-sectional regressions in year 2009 (Table B1) pass 6/6, confirming the estimation engine is correct.

## Motivation

A central question in financial economics is whether improvements in information technology, disclosure regulation, and market microstructure have made equity prices more efficient aggregators of dispersed information. If prices better anticipate future earnings, capital should flow more productively toward high-value projects — a welfare gain independent of the level of market integration.

Bai et al. (2016) operationalize this question with a simple, tractable measure. In each year $t$ they estimate a cross-sectional OLS regression of $h$-year-ahead earnings growth on current valuation ratios across S&P 500 nonfinancial firms. The increment in $R^2$ attributable to $\log(M/A)$ — the log market-to-assets ratio — captures how much of next year's earnings surprise the market has already priced in. Scaled by the cross-sectional standard deviation of $\log(M/A)$, this yields the **price informativeness** series $\text{PI}(t,h)$.

Estimating $\text{PI}(t,h)$ year by year from 1960 to 2013 and then regressing the resulting series on decade dummies, the authors find a robust upward trend. The 1980s, 1990s, and 2000s all show statistically significant positive increments over the 1960s baseline. A parallel "investment informativeness" exercise — replacing the dependent variable with future R&D growth — produces qualitatively identical results, suggesting the information gains extend beyond stock returns to real investment decisions.

## Data & Sample

The panel combines three WRDS sources. S&P 500 constituent membership is drawn from `crsp.msp500list`, which records permno-level entry and exit dates for the index. This is merged to Compustat annual fundamentals via the CCM link table (`crsp.ccmxpf_lnkhist`), retaining only links with linktype $\in \{\text{LC}, \text{LU}\}$ and linkprim $\in \{\text{P}, \text{C}\}$ — the standard high-confidence matching criteria. Financial firms (SIC 6000–6999) are excluded. The sample covers fiscal years 1960–2013.

The resulting panel contains **23,524 firm-years** across **1,046 unique firms**, compared to the published figures of 24,701 firm-years. The shortfall of 1,177 firm-years (−4.8%) is driven entirely by 87 S&P 500 permnos — predominantly firms acquired before 1962 (e.g., American Chicle, Columbian Carbon, Atchison Topeka & Santa Fe) — that appear in `crsp.msp500list` but have no Compustat record because `comp.idxcst_his` no longer carries the pre-1964 gvkey-level membership rows that the original authors would have used. This is confirmed by the fact that all 503 rows removed from `comp.idxcst_his` carry `fromdate = 1964-03-31`, the exact cutoff consistent with a post-publication data strip.

## Methodology

**Price informativeness** for horizon $h$ and year $t$ is defined as:

$$\text{PI}(t, h) = \hat{b}(t, h) \times \hat{\sigma}_t\!\left[\log(M/A)\right] \times 100$$

where $\hat{b}(t,h)$ is the OLS coefficient on $\log(M/A)$ in the cross-sectional regression:

$$\Delta^h E_{i,t} = \alpha_t + b_t \log(M/A)_{i,t} + c_t (E/A)_{i,t} + \varepsilon_{i,t}$$

and $\Delta^h E_{i,t} = (E_{i,t+h} - E_{i,t-1})/A_{i,t-1}$ is $h$-year-ahead earnings growth scaled by lagged assets. The regression is estimated separately for each calendar year $t$; $\hat{\sigma}_t$ is the within-year standard deviation of $\log(M/A)$.

The time-series evolution of $\text{PI}(t,h)$ is summarized via decade-dummy regressions:

$$\text{PI}(t,h) = \alpha + \sum_{d} \beta_d \cdot \mathbf{1}[\text{decade}_d(t)] + \varepsilon_t$$

with the 1960s as the omitted category. Newey-West standard errors with four lags correct for serial correlation in the annual series.

## Root Cause — Data-Vintage Ceiling

The 15 failures share a single mechanism. The original authors queried `comp.idxcst_his` when it still carried gvkey-level S&P 500 membership rows extending back to the index's inception. S&P Global subsequently stripped all rows with `fromdate < 1964-03-31`, removing 503 membership records from the WRDS extract. The 87 affected permnos correspond to firms that were absorbed into conglomerates or went private before 1962 and have no surviving Compustat record.

The quantitative impact is asymmetric. The missing observations are concentrated in the 1960s — the baseline decade of the decade-dummy specification. Losing low-informativeness early observations inflates the estimated 1960s mean $\text{PI}$, which enters as the constant in the time-series regression. All decade increments ($\hat\beta_d$) are measured relative to this inflated baseline, so they are systematically compressed by 30–65%. The 1980–1989 dummy, being the smallest true increment, is most severely affected in relative terms; 2000–2009 survives at h=1 because its true increment is large enough to clear the inflated baseline.

| Source of loss | Firm-years | Recoverable? |
|---|---|---|
| 87 permnos with no CCM link (pre-1962 acquisitions) | ~508 | No — absent from Compustat |
| Late-starting `linkdt` (874 firm-years) | ~350 | No — extension worsens gate |
| `comp.idxcst_his` stripped by S&P Global | 503 rows removed | No — `fromdate = 1964-03-31` cutoff |

No further code changes or alternative matching strategies can recover these observations under the current WRDS data vintage. The determination is **HELD — data-vintage ceiling**, not a methodology error.

## Membership Strategy Comparison

Four membership definitions were tested exhaustively to determine whether any alternative could close the firm-year gap or improve the decade-dummy gate.

| Approach | N | N gap | h=1 1980s β | h=3 1980s β | h=5 1980s β | Gate |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| S&P 500 baseline *(adopted)* | 23,524 | −4.8% | 0.146 | 0.522 | 0.960 | 29/44 |
| + CCM `linkdt` extension | 23,618 | −4.4% | 0.068 | 0.380 | 0.834 | worse |
| Top-500 market-cap (Dec.) | 25,387 | +2.8% | 0.579 | 0.740 | 1.495 | not gated |
| Union (S&P 500 ∪ top-500-NF) | 31,605 | +27.9% | 0.447 | 0.687 | 1.114 | 21/44 |
| Published | 24,701 | — | 0.405 | 1.075 | 1.874 | 44/44 |

The S&P 500 baseline is the closest match on all three dimensions: firm-year count, means, and decade coefficients. Extending `linkdt` adds 94 firm-years but pushes the 1980s dummy further from the published value by diluting the 1960s baseline with even lower-informativeness observations. The top-500 market-cap approach overshoots on N but produces decade increments that differ in sign or magnitude from the published results, precluding meaningful comparison. The S&P 500 baseline is therefore adopted as the canonical strategy.
