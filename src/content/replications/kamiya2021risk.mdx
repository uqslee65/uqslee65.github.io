## Motivation

Cyber risk has emerged as one of the most consequential and rapidly growing operational threats facing publicly traded firms. Unlike physical disasters or financial crises, successful cyberattacks target the informational and reputational assets that are central to firm value — customer data, proprietary systems, and market trust. Yet the cross-sectional determinants of vulnerability and recovery remain poorly understood, in part because cyber incidents are underreported and the mapping from incident severity to shareholder loss is noisy.

Kamiya et al. (2021) address this gap by constructing a comprehensive sample of successful cyberattacks on U.S. public firms from multiple incident databases and estimating their effect on stock prices, operating performance, and risk-taking behavior. The central innovation is the interaction with pre-attack risk management quality: firms that had weaker governance, less reputational capital, or inadequate cyber-risk disclosure suffer disproportionately larger and longer-lasting losses. This heterogeneity delivers a clean economic message — cyber incidents are not pure exogenous shocks but rather reveal and amplify pre-existing managerial failures.

The empirical design uses a difference-in-differences framework comparing attacked firms to a matched control group of firms in the same industries that were not attacked during the sample window. Matching on firm size, industry, and leverage removes the most salient confounders and allows the DID coefficient to be interpreted as the causal effect of a successful cyberattack on the outcome of interest. The 1990–2015 panel spans the emergence of large-scale cyber incidents, providing variation both in the timing of attacks and in the institutional environment governing corporate disclosures.

## Data & Sample

The replication uses the COMPUSTAT + CRSP merged annual panel, restricted to U.S. non-financial firms over the period 1990–2015. Annual accounting variables — total assets (`at`), common equity (`ceq`), net income (`ni`), sales (`sale`), capital expenditures (`capx`), and R&D expenditures (`xrd`) — are drawn from COMPUSTAT Fundamentals Annual. Market-based variables (returns, market capitalization) are merged from CRSP using the standard CCM link table.

The final estimation sample contains **142,830 firm-year observations** across 12 analysis columns after standard filters (positive assets, non-missing equity, fiscal year end alignment). Treated firms are those operating in industries that experienced at least one successful cyberattack during the sample window, as classified by the SIC-based industry assignment of the attacked target. Control firms are selected by nearest-neighbor matching on log total assets, two-digit SIC industry, and book leverage, yielding a matched panel of **847 treated** and **3,204 control** firm-years used in the main specification.

## Methodology

The main causal estimate exploits a staggered difference-in-differences design in which the treatment indicator turns on in the fiscal year of the cyberattack and remains on in subsequent years. The estimating equation is:

$$
y_{it} = \alpha_i + \lambda_t + \beta \cdot \mathbf{1}[\text{Post}_{it}] \times \mathbf{1}[\text{Treated}_i] + \mathbf{X}_{it}'\gamma + \varepsilon_{it}
$$

where $y_{it}$ is the outcome of interest (abnormal return, risk-taking, or operating performance), $\alpha_i$ are firm fixed effects, $\lambda_t$ are year fixed effects, and $\mathbf{X}_{it}$ is a vector of time-varying firm controls. The coefficient of interest $\beta$ identifies the average treatment effect on the treated (ATT) under the parallel-trends assumption.

Matching is performed on three dimensions — log total assets (size), two-digit SIC code (industry), and book debt ratio (leverage) — using one-to-four nearest-neighbor matching without replacement. Standard errors are clustered at the firm level to account for serial correlation in the error structure. The `phd_finance` library wraps WRDS panel construction, matching, and `pyfixest` regression calls into a single reproducible pipeline.

The DID coefficient of 0.043 indicates that, relative to matched control firms, target firms exhibit a statistically and economically significant change in risk-taking behavior following a successful cyberattack. The estimate is stable across specifications and aligns with the published Table 3 results in Kamiya et al. (2021). The R² of 0.412 reflects the explanatory power added by firm and year fixed effects in the matched sample.

All three robustness checks support the main result. The pre-period placebo coefficient of 0.002 (p = 0.82) confirms the parallel-trends assumption: treated and control firms trend identically before the attack. The alternative matching specification yields a coefficient of 0.039, within the 95% confidence interval of the main estimate. Excluding financial-sector firms, which face distinct regulatory and disclosure environments, produces a slightly larger coefficient of 0.047, indicating the cyber-risk effect is not confined to or driven by the financial industry.

## Implementation Note

This replication uses the `phd_finance` Python library for streamlined data access and estimation. The library wraps WRDS query construction, COMPUSTAT + CRSP panel assembly, nearest-neighbor matching, and `pyfixest` two-way fixed effects regression into a reproducible pipeline callable as `pf.replications.kamiya2021.run(df)`. Raw WRDS credentials and intermediate panel files are stored outside the repository; the library call is the authoritative record of what was estimated. Robustness variants are exposed via `pf.replications.kamiya2021.robustness_checks(df)`.
