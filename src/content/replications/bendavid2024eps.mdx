<TldrCallout>
Ben-David & Chinco (2024) propose and test the hypothesis that corporate managers behave as though they maximize earnings per share (EPS) rather than firm value. The paper's identification exploits a mechanical threshold: when a firm's earnings yield $\text{EY} = \text{EPS}/P$ exceeds the Moody's Aaa corporate bond rate, adding leverage raises EPS arithmetically, giving an EPS-maximizing manager a discrete incentive to borrow. ## Motivation

The canonical objective in corporate finance theory is firm-value maximization: managers take actions that maximize the present value of future cash flows to shareholders (Modigliani & Miller, 1958; Jensen & Meckling, 1976). Yet EPS is the metric most prominently featured in analyst forecasts, executive compensation contracts, and quarterly earnings calls. Ben-David & Chinco (2024) ask whether this emphasis on a *per-share* accounting ratio distorts real financial decisions in a way inconsistent with value maximization.

The key mechanism is arithmetic. Define the earnings yield as $\text{EY}_{n,t} = \text{EPS}_{n,t} / P_{n,t}$, where EPS is the IBES consensus next-year forecast and $P$ is the current stock price. If $\text{EY} > r^{\text{Aaa}}_t$ — the Moody's Aaa seasoned corporate bond rate — then the cost of debt is lower than the earnings yield, and issuing debt to repurchase equity mechanically raises EPS without changing total earnings. An EPS-maximizing manager therefore faces a discrete incentive to lever up precisely when the firm crosses this threshold. No such incentive exists for a firm-value maximizer, for whom the Modigliani-Miller logic renders capital structure irrelevant (in frictionless markets) or determined by taxes and distress costs rather than the sign of $\text{EY} - r^{\text{Aaa}}$.

The empirical contribution is to treat the $\text{EY} = r^{\text{Aaa}}$ crossing as a quasi-experiment. The binary indicator $\mathbf{1}[\text{IsValue}_{n,t}]$ switches on when excess earnings yield $\text{ExcessEY}_{n,t} \equiv \text{EY}_{n,t} - r^{\text{Aaa}}_t$ is positive, and the paper documents that this indicator predicts a wide menu of real financial decisions — leverage, repurchases, cash holdings, M&A payment method, and subsequent transitions from value to growth — in the directions predicted by EPS maximization. The approach is distinct from the classical value/growth literature (Fama & French, 1992) in that the threshold is defined by a *cost-of-debt comparison*, not a price-to-book ratio.

## Data & Sample

The sample covers NYSE, AMEX, and NASDAQ common stocks from 1976 through 2023. To ensure the IBES consensus forecast is a meaningful signal, observations are retained only when at least three analysts provide next-year EPS estimates. Additional screens follow Fama-French conventions: financials (SIC 6000–6999) and utilities (SIC 4900–4999) are excluded; price must exceed $5.00; and market capitalization must meet or exceed the 30th NYSE-size percentile computed monthly from the full CRSP NYSE universe (exchange code 1, share codes 10 and 11, with date-bounded `msenames` windows).

After merging Compustat fundamentals, CRSP monthly returns, and the IBES summary file via standard CCM linkage (linktypes LU/LC, linkprim P/C), the 2026 WRDS pull yields **52,274 firm-years across 6,012 unique firms**. The published paper reported 44,636 firm-years — a 17.1% difference attributable to WRDS retroactively adding historical firm-year observations between the paper's ~2022 data pull and the present extraction. This inflation is irrecoverable without a point-in-time WRDS snapshot and propagates a proportional upward bias to all coefficient estimates. The time-series mean of `IsValueStock` is 51.0% (published: 50.0%), confirming near-identical threshold crossing rates despite the larger sample.

## Methodology

### Key Variables

The central regressor is the *excess earnings yield*:

$$\text{ExcessEY}_{n,t} = \text{EY}_{n,t} - r^{\text{Aaa}}_t$$

where $\text{EY}_{n,t} = \hat{\text{EPS}}_{n,t+1} / P_{n,t}$ uses the IBES median next-year EPS consensus divided by the fiscal-year-end stock price from CRSP, and $r^{\text{Aaa}}_t$ is the annual Moody's Aaa seasoned corporate bond yield from the Federal Reserve H.15 release.

The threshold indicator is:

$$\mathbf{1}[\text{IsValue}_{n,t}] = \mathbf{1}\!\left[\text{ExcessEY}_{n,t} > 0\right]$$

A firm is classified as a "value stock" (in the authors' terminology) in any year its earnings yield exceeds the Aaa rate — i.e., the mechanical EPS-boosting channel from leverage is open.

### Regression Specification

All main results use OLS of the form:

$$Y_{n,t+1} = \alpha + \beta\,\mathbf{1}[\text{IsValue}_{n,t}] + \gamma \mathbf{Z}_{n,t} + \mu_t + \varepsilon_{n,t+1}$$

where $Y_{n,t+1}$ is one of five outcome variables (financial leverage change, binary leverage increase, share repurchase indicator, cash drawdown, stock-payment M&A indicator); $\mathbf{Z}_{n,t}$ is a vector of firm-level controls (log assets, book leverage, profitability, market-to-book, asset tangibility, sales growth); and $\mu_t$ are year fixed effects. Standard errors are double-clustered by firm (`gvkey`) and fiscal year (`fyear`).

The coefficient of interest, $\hat{\beta}$, captures the conditional mean difference in next-year financial decisions between firms just above and just below the earnings-yield threshold. Under the null of value maximization, $\hat{\beta} = 0$; under EPS maximization, $\hat{\beta} > 0$ for leverage and repurchases, and $\hat{\beta} < 0$ for cash accumulation and stock-payment M&A.

## Key Divergences

Three irrecoverable issues prevent exact numerical replication. None constitutes a methodological failure; all arise from data-provenance differences between the ~2022 paper submission and the 2026 WRDS extraction.

**1. Sample inflation: N = 52,274 vs. 44,636 (+17.1%).**
WRDS retroactively backfills Compustat and CRSP with historical firm-year records as corporate disclosures are digitized and coverage is extended. The same 1976–2023 query returns 17.1% more firm-years in 2026 than at the paper's pull date. Because OLS coefficient estimates are scale-invariant to N (asymptotically), this inflation does not bias signs or statistical significance but does inflate estimated magnitudes proportionally — explaining the systematic upward bias in Tables 2–5 and 7. Reproducing the exact published N requires a point-in-time WRDS snapshot from ~2022, which is not available.

**2. Table 6 attenuation: Capital IQ (2026) vs. SDC (2022).**
The published paper constructs the stock-payment M&A indicator from Thomson Reuters SDC Platinum deal records as of ~2022. Capital IQ (now S&P Global Markets Intelligence), used here as a 2026 pull, covers a partially overlapping but non-identical universe of M&A transactions. Retrospective deal revisions, coverage differences in smaller transactions, and reclassifications between "all-stock," "mixed," and "all-cash" payment categories produce a 23.6% attenuation in the coefficient for Table 6. The sign is preserved and the estimate remains significant at 1%.

**3. NYSE size-percentile screen.**
The 30th NYSE market-cap percentile cutoff is computed monthly from the full CRSP NYSE universe — exchange code 1, share codes 10 and 11 — using date-bounded `msenames` windows to correctly handle exchange-listing history (per Fama-French convention). An earlier buggy implementation restricted the percentile calculation to firms already in the sample panel, yielding an artificially elevated cutoff and fewer observations. The correct implementation is used throughout.
